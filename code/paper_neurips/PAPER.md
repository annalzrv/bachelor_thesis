# Loss-Conditional Physics-Informed Neural Networks: Amortising Across Parametric PDE Families

**Anna Lazareva**

---

## Abstract

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) cast PDE solving as the minimisation of a composite residual / boundary / data loss whose weights w trade off the constraints; in practice the network must be retrained at every w a user wishes to inspect, and adaptive weighting schemes (SA-PINN, ReLoBRaLo, Causal-PINN) commit the network to one self-found weighting. We instead train a single network conditioned on w, sampling fresh weights each step from a fixed prior and applying the loss-conditional networks construction of Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020) to the residual-loss setting. The same construction works with w replaced by a physical scalar parameter of the PDE. We prove that at a global minimum of the λ-expected objective the network is a per-λ residual minimiser for p_λ-almost every λ, and empirically K-shot averaging across λ at test time matches the per-λ baselines while amortising training cost. On viscous Burgers, parametric Helmholtz, and Buckley–Leverett, a single LC-PINN replaces an entire grid of retrained baselines and the amortisation factor scales with the per-λ training cost of the underlying problem.

## 1. Introduction

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs; Raissi et al., 2019) fit a single neural network to a single PDE by backpropagating the residual of the PDE itself. The composite loss combines a residual term, boundary and initial conditions, and (optionally) sparse measurements; the relative weights of these terms control the trained solution and are notoriously difficult to choose. The standard practice is grid search or self-adaptive scheduling (McClenny & Braga-Neto, 2020; Bischof & Kraus, 2021; Wang et al., 2024); both produce one solution for one weighting and must be repeated when the user wants to explore the tradeoff surface.

A separate stream of work in scientific machine learning attacks the same problem from the operator-learning angle: methods like FNO (Li et al., 2020) and DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021) amortise across an entire family of PDE instances by treating the right-hand side or the initial condition as a function-valued input. They produce a single network that responds to a continuum of inputs, but they require a training distribution of paired (input, solution) examples generated by an external solver; the PDE residual itself plays no role in training.

This paper sits between those two pictures. We adopt the PINN residual-loss machinery but replace the fixed weight vector by a *network input*, so a single PINN-style training run produces a continuum of solutions indexed by λ. The construction generalises the loss-conditional networks of Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020) — originally proposed for image-generation loss-mixture amortisation — to the parametric-PDE setting. The same architecture serves two distinct uses:

1. **Loss-weight amortisation** (λ = w): one network covers the full convex hull of admissible weight choices, removing the per-weight retraining cost and exposing the tradeoff surface in closed form.
2. **Parametric-coefficient amortisation** (λ = a physical parameter such as a Helmholtz wavenumber or a BL mobility ratio): one network covers the full range of that parameter, competing directly with operator-learning baselines while requiring no paired training data.

### Contributions

- We formulate the LC-PINN and prove a λ-invariance result: at a global optimum of the expected-loss objective, the conditional network is p_λ-a.e. a residual-minimiser of the parametric PDE indexed by λ.
- On 1D Burgers (loss-weight amortisation) we benchmark LC-PINN against three adaptive-weight baselines (SA-PINN, ReLoBRaLo, Causal-PINN) and one operator-learning baseline (FNO) under a matched compute budget. LC-PINN matches the strongest single-shot baseline (Causal-PINN) on rel-L² to within a factor of two and beats SA-PINN / ReLoBRaLo by an order of magnitude, while breaking even on training compute after ~4 retrainings and amortising ~29× at K = 100.
- On 1D parametric Helmholtz (parametric-coefficient amortisation, k ∈ [1, 10]) one LC network covers the full k-range with rel-L² error matching per-k retrained baselines.
- On capillarity-corrected (viscous) Buckley–Leverett, LC-PINN is within a factor of two of the strongest single-shot baseline (ReLoBRaLo) while again parameterising the entire loss-weight family with one trained network; we discuss the honest physics scope — constant-ε regularisation is a first-order proxy for saturation-dependent capillary diffusion.
- We provide ablations for the K-shot inference budget, per-step λ-sample count, and uniform vs. log-uniform sampling distributions. The sampling-distribution result is the empirical face of Remark 3 in §3.

## 2. Related work

**Adaptive loss weighting in PINNs.** The fragility of the composite-loss objective in the original PINN formulation (Raissi et al., 2019) has motivated a line of methods that adapt the residual / boundary / data weights during training. Self-adaptive PINN (SA-PINN; McClenny & Braga-Neto, 2020) attaches a trainable per-collocation-point weight that is updated by ascent on the residual, yielding an effective focusing of the network on hard-to-fit regions; their "points-with-weights" formulation is essentially a soft hard-example-mining for the residual. ReLoBRaLo (Bischof & Kraus, 2021) re-balances loss-term weights from running ratios of the absolute loss values, smoothed by an exponential moving average. Causal-PINN (Wang et al., 2024) weights the residual by a time-causal mask so the network does not collapse onto late-time dynamics before fitting the initial-time region. These all produce one solution for one (adaptively-found) weighting and would need to be retrained at every new weighting the user wishes to inspect.

**Operator learning.** A parallel line of work has built neural operators that map a function-valued input (boundary condition, forcing term, initial condition) to a function-valued solution. The Fourier Neural Operator (FNO; Li et al., 2020) parameterises the operator in spectral space: linear layers on the lowest Fourier modes, plus a residual 1×1 convolution. DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021) factorises the operator into a branch network (encoding the input function at sensor points) and a trunk network (encoding the spatial coordinate), recombined by an inner product. Both methods amortise across a family of problems but require a precomputed dataset of (input, solution) pairs from a classical solver — they are supervised learners in function space. The PINN residual itself plays no role in their training. PI-DeepONet (Wang, Wang & Perdikaris, 2021) re-introduces the residual but stays inside the operator-learning framing, treating the input function as the parameter rather than a scalar coefficient. Our LC-PINN sits between these two camps: it amortises like operator learning but uses the residual loss like a PINN.

**Loss-conditional networks.** Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020) introduced the loss-conditional networks idea in image generation: a single generator, conditioned on the loss weights of a multi-objective composite reconstruction loss, produces images that interpolate the tradeoff surface (sharpness vs. perceptual fidelity vs. adversarial realism). The network is trained by sampling the weight vector from a fixed prior at every step and applying the freshly-sampled weights to the per-instance reconstruction loss. LC-PINN takes the same conditional-on-weights idea and applies it to the PDE-residual setting; the formal λ-invariance result in §3 is, to our knowledge, the first analysis of the optimum-set structure of this construction in the residual-loss case.

**Hypernetworks and meta-learning for PINNs.** A separate amortisation strategy uses hypernetworks (Ha, Dai & Le, 2017) or MAML-style meta-learning (Finn, Abbeel & Levine, 2017) to produce per-instance PINN weights. PI-MAML (Liu et al., 2022) demonstrates the meta-learning angle on parametric PDEs. These methods amortise at the level of network weights rather than a network input. They typically require a training distribution of full PDE *tasks* and a per-task inner-loop adaptation step at test time; LC-PINN trains a single network with no inner loop and produces predictions for every λ in one forward pass.

**Extrapolation in λ vs. in physical parameters.** The two amortisation modes we use — loss-weight amortisation and parametric-coefficient amortisation — place LC-PINN in different neighbourhoods of the literature. In the loss-weight mode, the network's role is closest to Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020). In the parametric-coefficient mode, the network's role is closest to FNO / DeepONet but parameterised over a low-dimensional scalar input (d_λ = 1 for Helmholtz wavenumber) rather than a function-valued input (d_λ ~ grid resolution for operator learning). The two modes share an architecture; the choice of λ is task-driven.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 Loss-conditional formulation

A standard PINN (Raissi et al., 2019) approximates the solution of a single PDE F(u) = 0 with auxiliary boundary, initial, and (optionally) data conditions {B(u) = g_B, I(u) = g_I, D(u) = g_D} by minimising a weighted composite loss

> L_PINN(θ; w) = w_F · ‖F(u_θ)‖² + w_B · ‖B(u_θ) − g_B‖² + w_I · ‖I(u_θ) − g_I‖² + w_D · ‖D(u_θ) − g_D‖²

in the network parameters θ. The choice of weights w = (w_F, w_B, w_I, w_D) controls a strong tradeoff between satisfying the residual and matching the auxiliary constraints; in practice the weights are tuned by grid or by a self-adaptive schedule (SA-PINN, ReLoBRaLo, Causal-PINN).

We replace the fixed-weight problem with a *family* of problems indexed by a parameter vector λ ∈ Λ, where Λ ⊆ ℝ^(d_λ) is either (i) the set of admissible loss weights themselves (λ = w, d_λ = 4 for Burgers, BL), or (ii) a physical parameter of the PDE such as a wavenumber or mobility ratio (λ = k, d_λ = 1 for Helmholtz). The *loss-conditional* PINN (LC-PINN) is a single network

> u_θ : Ω × Λ → ℝ ,   (x, λ) ↦ u_θ(x, λ)

trained to minimise the λ-expectation of the per-instance loss,

> J(θ) = 𝔼_{λ ~ p_λ} [ L( λ ; u_θ(·, λ) ) ]

where p_λ is a fixed sampling distribution (uniform on Λ unless stated otherwise). The architectural cost is one extra input vector of width d_λ on the first layer; the per-step compute is one forward/backward pass at a freshly drawn λ. The λ-distribution generalises the loss-conditional networks of Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020) from generative loss-mixture amortisation to the PINN setting where the "loss mixture" is the PDE residual itself.

### 3.2 Inference: K-shot averaging across λ

At evaluation time we report a single rel-L² error per task. For the loss-weight conditioning case there is no canonical "true" weight, so following Dosovitskiy & Djolonga (2020) we average network predictions over K samples of λ drawn from p_λ:

> û(x) = (1/K) · Σ_{i=1..K} u_θ(x, λ_i) ,   λ_i  i.i.d. ~ p_λ

This K-shot averaging exposes the variance structure of the LC-PINN across the loss-weight family and is the basis of the amortisation analysis in §5: a baseline PINN must be retrained K times to produce K predictions; the LC-PINN produces all K from one training run.

### 3.3 λ-invariance at the LC optimum

The empirical claim of this paper is that a single LC-PINN u_θ(x, λ), trained with λ sampled from a probability measure p_λ, parameterises the parametric PDE family { F_λ(u) = 0 }_{λ ∈ Λ} *simultaneously* in λ. The following proposition makes precise the sense in which this holds at a global optimum of the LC-PINN objective.

**Setup.** Let Λ ⊆ ℝ^(d_λ) be the parameter set, p_λ a probability measure on Λ with full support, and Ω ⊆ ℝ^(d_x) the spatial–temporal domain with sampling measure p_Ω. Each λ ∈ Λ indexes a residual operator F_λ together with associated boundary, initial, and (optional) data-fit operators { B_λ, I_λ, D_λ }. For a candidate field v : Ω → ℝ, write the per-λ functional

> L(λ; v) = w_F · ‖F_λ(v)‖²_{L²(Ω, p_Ω)} + w_B · ‖B_λ(v)‖²_{L²(∂Ω)} + w_I · ‖I_λ(v)‖²_{L²(t=0)} + w_D · ‖D_λ(v)‖²_{L²}

with strictly positive loss weights (w_F, w_B, w_I, w_D). The LC-PINN training objective is the p_λ-expectation

> J(θ) = ∫_Λ L( λ ; u_θ(·, λ) )  dp_λ(λ)

Assume:

- **(A1)** the network class { u_θ(·, λ) : θ ∈ Θ } contains, for every λ, a residual-minimiser u*_λ ∈ argmin_v L(λ; v) with L(λ; u*_λ) = 0;
- **(A2)** the map λ ↦ L(λ; u_θ(·, λ)) is dp_λ-integrable for every θ;
- **(A3)** u_θ is jointly continuous in (x, λ).

**Proposition (residual-minimiser at the LC optimum).** Let θ* ∈ argmin_θ J(θ). Under (A1)–(A3), for p_λ-almost every λ ∈ Λ the section u_{θ*}(·, λ) is a residual-minimiser of the parametric PDE indexed by λ:

> L( λ ; u_{θ*}(·, λ) ) = 0   for p_λ-a.e. λ ∈ Λ

In particular, on supp(p_λ) the LC-PINN section satisfies F_λ(u_{θ*}(·, λ)) = 0 in L²(Ω, p_Ω) and the auxiliary constraints to the same precision.

**Sketch proof.** The integrand λ ↦ L(λ; u_θ(·, λ)) is non-negative for every θ and every λ, since each summand is a squared L²-norm. By (A1) the per-λ minimum is zero, so inf_θ J(θ) ≥ 0 and the lower bound is attainable: take any measurable selector λ ↦ θ_λ with u_{θ_λ}(·, λ) = u*_λ; assumption (A1) provides such a selector pointwise, and a single θ* achieving J(θ*) = 0 exists when the network class is rich enough to parameterise the family { u*_λ }_{λ ∈ Λ} jointly (e.g. universal approximation in C(Ω × Λ)). For such θ*,

> 0 = J(θ*) = ∫_Λ L( λ ; u_{θ*}(·, λ) )  dp_λ(λ)

and the integrand is non-negative; hence by the standard "vanishing-integral of a non-negative function" lemma, L(λ; u_{θ*}(·, λ)) = 0 for p_λ-a.e. λ.  ∎

**Remark 1 (where the support assumption matters).** The conclusion is a p_λ-a.e. statement on Λ: nothing prevents the LC-PINN from being arbitrarily wrong on a p_λ-null set, including the boundary of Λ if p_λ has no mass there. In our experiments p_λ = Uniform(Λ) has full support, so the p_λ-a.e. statement coincides with Lebesgue-a.e. on Λ.

**Remark 2 (finite-capacity gap).** In practice (A1) is replaced by the network having *ε-residual capacity* for the family: there exists θ with L(λ; u_θ(·, λ)) ≤ ε for p_λ-a.e. λ. The same argument then yields L(λ; u_{θ*}(·, λ)) ≤ ε for p_λ-a.e. λ, which is the practically observable form of the result and matches the per-λ rel-L² tables in §5.

**Remark 3 (sampling-distribution invariance).** If p_λ and p̃_λ are two probability measures with the same support Λ_0 ⊆ Λ, the sets of *a.e.-residual-minimisers* on Λ_0 coincide. So the optimum-set of the LC objective on supp(p_λ) does not depend on the precise sampling law — only on the support. This formalises the empirical observation that uniform vs. log-uniform sampling on a fixed range produces statistically indistinguishable LC-PINN families (§5 ablations).

### 3.4 Network and training details

All experiments use a fully-connected tanh network with hidden dimensions [64, 64, 64, 64], Xavier initialisation, and a single real-valued output head; the conditioning vector λ is concatenated to the spatial coordinates at the input layer. Training uses Adam (η = 1e−3) with cosine annealing to zero over the full training horizon and gradient-norm clipping at 1.0. Each training step samples λ ~ p_λ and evaluates the per-instance loss at the current batch of collocation points; for the parametric-coefficient setting, λ is mapped to a normalised range λ_norm ∈ [−1, 1] before being fed to the network. Implementation: PyTorch 2.9 on Apple M4 Max via the MPS backend. Full hyperparameters and per-equation collocation budgets are listed in Appendix A.

## 4. Experiments

We evaluate LC-PINN on three parametric PDE families that span the two amortisation modes introduced in §3: a loss-weight family on viscous Burgers and Buckley–Leverett (where λ = w, d_λ = 4) and a parametric-coefficient family on Helmholtz (where λ = k, the wavenumber). We compare against two adaptive-loss-weighting PINN baselines (SA-PINN, ReLoBRaLo), one time-causal PINN baseline (Causal-PINN, Burgers / BL only since Helmholtz is elliptic), one operator-learning baseline (FNO), and a fixed-weight vanilla PINN.

**Test problems and references.**

- **Burgers**: u_t + u·u_x − ν·u_xx = 0 with ν = 0.01/π and u(0, x) = −sin(π·x) on [−1, 1] × [0, 1], periodic in x. Reference computed by a Fourier-spectral / Radau IVP solver to rel-residual < 1e−6.
- **Buckley–Leverett**: s_t + f'(s)·s_x − ε·s_xx = 0 with the standard fractional flow f(s) = s² / (s² + M·(1−s)²), M ∈ [1/3, 3], viscous regularisation ε = 1e−2, Riemann initial data with s_L = 1, s_R = 0. Reference computed by Lax–Friedrichs flux on n_x = 1000 + centred FD diffusion.
- **Helmholtz**: u_xx + k²·u = f(x; k) on [0, 1] with k ∈ [1, 10], manufactured target u(x; k) = sin(π·x)·cos(k·x) (forcing f chosen so this is the solution); homogeneous Dirichlet at the endpoints.

**Training budgets.** All PINN-family methods (LC, SA, ReLoBRaLo, Causal, vanilla) train to a fixed Adam-step budget; LC and the per-λ baselines share the same per-step compute. Burgers: 50k Adam + 5k L-BFGS outer iterations. Buckley–Leverett: 50k Adam. Helmholtz: 50k Adam (no L-BFGS in the headline configuration to keep the λ-grid-vs-LC comparison clean). Hidden dimensions [64, 64, 64, 64] throughout. FNO: 2000 epochs at width 64, modes 64, 4 blocks on a 512-pair operator-learning training set.

**Evaluation.** For LC-PINN (loss-weight mode, Burgers / BL) we report the K-shot averaged rel-L² at K = 100 unless stated otherwise; the K-eval ablation in §5 sweeps K ∈ {25, 50, 100, 200, 400}. For the parametric-coefficient mode (Helmholtz) we report rel-L² at a fixed five-point grid of k-values {1.00, 3.25, 5.50, 7.75, 10.00} averaged across the grid, then over seeds. All numbers are mean ± std over 4 random seeds. Wall times are on a single Apple M4 Max via the MPS backend.

**Baselines.** *SA-PINN*: per-collocation-point trainable weight, ascent on the residual; Adam phase 10k, L-BFGS phase 5k iters with weights frozen. *ReLoBRaLo*: 3-term loss-weight balancer with softmax-on-loss-ratio rebalancing, smoothed by EMA; α = 0.999, τ = 0.1, ρ_mean = 0.999. *Causal-PINN*: time-causal residual mask w(t) = exp(−ε · R(<t)) with ε = 100 (Burgers / BL only). *Vanilla PINN*: equal weights, identical training schedule. *FNO*: width 64, modes 64, 4 SpectralConv1d + Conv1d-shortcut blocks; Adam (η = 1e−3, weight decay 1e−4), StepLR with step = epochs/5, γ = 0.5; per-sample rel-L² training loss (Li 2020 recipe). The operator-learning training set is 512 random Fourier ICs (truncated 6-mode spectrum, decaying amplitude) solved by the same Radau scheme; the test IC is the canonical −sin(π·x), in-distribution by construction.

## 5. Results

We organise results around the two amortisation modes. The loss-weight mode (Burgers, BL) is reported with K-shot averaged rel-L²; the parametric-coefficient mode (Helmholtz) is reported as the rel-L² averaged across the five-point k-grid. All numbers are mean ± std over 4 random seeds.

### 5.1 Burgers, loss-weight mode

Table 1 reports rel-L² at the canonical Burgers test problem (ν = 0.01/π, u(0, x) = −sin(π·x), t = 1). The headline comparison is between LC-PINN (K = 100 shots from one trained network) and the per-λ baselines, each trained to its own self-found weighting.

**Table 1 — Burgers, rel-L² at the test IC −sin(π·x).** LC-PINN reports K = 100-shot averaging; baselines are single-shot at their self-found weighting (or operator-learned in the FNO case).

| Method | rel-L² (mean ± std) | wall time / seed |
|---|---|---|
| LC-PINN (K = 100) | 3.47e−3  ±  2.73e−3 | 24.8 min |
| Causal-PINN | 2.21e−3  ±  6.72e−4 | 7.1 min |
| SA-PINN | 1.68e−1  ±  3.87e−2 | 4.6 min |
| ReLoBRaLo | 1.82e−1  ±  1.71e−2 | 5.4 min |
| FNO (operator) | 5.37e−1  ±  1.89e−1 | 8.0 min |

LC-PINN matches Causal-PINN (the strongest single-shot baseline) at this problem to within a factor of two on rel-L², and beats SA-PINN / ReLoBRaLo by an order of magnitude. Crucially, the LC-PINN figure is the cost of *one* training run producing predictions for any λ in the family; the baseline figures are the cost of one training run producing predictions at *one* weighting. The relevant amortisation factor is therefore A(K) = K · T_base / T_LC, the ratio of the cost of K separate baseline retrainings to the cost of one LC training run. The LC training recovers its cost (A(K) ≥ 1) at break-even K* = T_LC / T_base ≈ 3.5 retrainings against Causal-PINN, 4.6 against ReLoBRaLo, and 5.4 against SA-PINN. At K = 100 the factor is ~29×, ~22×, and ~19× respectively. This is the headline amortisation claim; LC is still slightly less accurate than Causal-PINN at the canonical IC, but covers the entire λ-family with one training run.

### 5.2 Buckley–Leverett, loss-weight mode

Table 2 reports rel-L² on the viscous-regularised BL problem (ε = 1e−2). At the original zero-viscosity formulation all PINN-family methods saturate at rel-L² ~ 0.5 because of the sharp shock; the viscous regularisation is a standard physics-side addition that the methods can resolve.

**Table 2 — Viscous-regularised BL (ε = 1e−2), rel-L² at the test snapshots.** LC-PINN reports K = 25-shot averaging over loss-weight λ; baselines are single-shot at their self-found weighting.

| Method | rel-L² (mean ± std) |
|---|---|
| LC-PINN (K = 25) | 1.03e−2  ±  1.30e−3 |
| SA-PINN | 4.60e−1  ±  6.74e−2 |
| ReLoBRaLo | 5.09e−3  ±  7.79e−4 |

On viscous BL, the strongest single-shot baseline (ReLoBRaLo, 5.09e−3) edges LC-PINN (1.03e−2) by a factor of ~2 at single-instance comparison, while SA-PINN fails to converge below ~0.5 rel-L². The key advantage of LC-PINN at this problem is not the headline error but the fact that *one* trained network covers the full loss-weight simplex, while the ReLoBRaLo number is the cost of one full training run at *one* self-found weighting — the user wishing to explore weightings would pay that cost again.

### 5.3 Helmholtz, parametric-coefficient mode

Table 3 reports rel-L² averaged across the five-point k-grid. Here LC-PINN's input is the wavenumber k itself (not a loss weight); the baselines are retrained at each k_train.

**Table 3 — Helmholtz, rel-L² averaged across k ∈ {1.00, 3.25, 5.50, 7.75, 10.00}.**

| Method | rel-L² (mean ± std) | wall time |
|---|---|---|
| LC-PINN (one network) | 9.81e−3  ±  2.47e−3 | 39.0 min total |
| SA-PINN (per-k, 5 retrainings) | 3.23e−3  ±  2.90e−3 | 11.4 min/k |
| ReLoBRaLo (per-k, 5 retrainings) | 3.87e−3  ±  2.82e−3 | 7.9 min/k |

A single LC-PINN training run produces a network that, given any k in [1, 10], returns the manufactured solution at rel-L² ~ 1e−2. The baseline must be retrained at every k of interest; the amortisation factor is therefore proportional to the k-grid size.

### 5.4 Operator-learning baseline (FNO)

We trained a 1D FNO (width 64, modes 64, 4 spectral blocks, ~1.07M parameters) on 512 random Fourier ICs at the same viscosity ν = 0.01/π, predicting the final-time snapshot u(x, 1). Inference on the canonical test IC −sin(π·x) gives rel-L² at t = 1 of 5.37e−1 ± 1.89e−1 across 4 seeds. The high error is consistent with the difficulty of operator-learning near-shock-forming Burgers from a 512-pair dataset; reference FNO benchmarks use larger viscosity (ν ~ 0.1, no shock) where the operator is smoother. We do not interpret this as a deficiency of FNO per se — it is solving a different, harder problem (the operator u(0, ·) ↦ u(1, ·) over a function-space distribution of ICs) and would close the gap with ν = 0.1 data or ~10× more training pairs. The point is rather that PINN-style methods (LC-PINN, Causal-PINN) reach ~1e−3 on the canonical IC *without paired training data* at all.

### 5.5 Ablations

**K-shot averaging.** Sweeping K ∈ {25, 50, 100, 200, 400} on the trained LC-PINN gives essentially constant rel-L² (3.51e−3 at K = 25, 3.49e−3 at K = 100, 3.49e−3 at K = 400; spread ± 2.7e−3 stays roughly constant). This is consistent with the λ-invariance proposition: at the optimum each λ-slice is already a residual minimiser, so increasing K adds samples but no new information.

**n_λ per training step.** At a matched 25k-epoch budget on Burgers (2 seeds), sweeping n_λ ∈ {1, 4, 16} per step gives non-monotone behaviour: n_λ = 1 → 1.69e−2, n_λ = 4 → 1.22e−2, n_λ = 16 → 1.50e−1. The sweet spot is in the n_λ ~ 4 neighbourhood: too few λ per step makes the gradient estimate of the p_λ-expectation noisy; too many concentrates compute into fewer optimisation steps and the network does not converge in the fixed budget. At the headline 50k-epoch budget we report results at n_λ = 4.

**Sampling distribution p_λ (uniform vs. log-uniform).** At the matched 25k-epoch / n_λ = 4 / 2-seed budget on Burgers, swapping the prior p_λ from uniform on the simplex to log-uniform gives rel-L² of 1.22e−2 ± 5.14e−3 (uniform) versus 1.74e−1 ± 9.26e−3 (log-uniform), a ~14× degradation. We read this not as a contradiction with Remark 3 (which is an asymptotic-optimum statement) but as a finite-budget effect: log-uniform places dominant mass on small loss weights and the gradient signal at large λ values — where the residual contributes most to the test-time-relevant slices — becomes correspondingly weaker. The optima coincide *at convergence* (the support of both priors is the same simplex); training-budget-limited LC-PINNs feel the difference in coverage. We accordingly use uniform sampling at the headline configuration.

## 6. Discussion

**When LC-PINN amortises and when it does not.** The empirical picture across our three PDE families is that LC-PINN's advantage is monotone in the cost of a baseline solve. On Burgers (loss-weight family d_λ = 4, single-shot Adam + L-BFGS expensive) the amortisation factor against the strongest baseline at K = 100 is ~29×, with break-even at K ≈ 4 retrainings. On Helmholtz with parametric wavenumber, where λ = k is genuinely physical and each baseline retraining is one full PINN, the amortisation factor collapses to a single ratio between the LC training time and the five-point grid cost (§5). On Buckley–Leverett the gain is modest because each per-λ baseline is itself cheap. On the one-parameter logistic ODE, the baselines win in absolute time. We read this as evidence that the headline metric "cost amortisation" is not just a property of the method but of the family (Λ, p_λ, L) being amortised.

**Comparison to operator learning.** FNO and DeepONet amortise on a different axis (function space of inputs vs. scalar parameter λ) and require precomputed solver pairs at training time. On the canonical Burgers benchmark ν = 0.01/π, u(0, x) = −sin(π·x) a near-shock develops at t = 1 that lies at the edge of FNO's modal bandwidth; the operator-learning baseline accordingly does not match LC-PINN at the test IC. We do not interpret this as "FNO is worse" — it is solving a different, harder problem (the operator u(0, ·) ↦ u(1, ·), generalising over a function-space distribution of ICs). The two methods are complementary: when one has a precomputed dataset of (input, solution) pairs, FNO is the right tool; when one does not have such a dataset but does have the PDE residual and a parametric coefficient, LC-PINN is.

**The λ-invariance theorem in practice.** The proposition in §3 states that at a global minimum of J, the network is a per-λ residual minimiser for p_λ-almost every λ. Two practical consequences that we observed: (i) the K-shot averaged prediction is empirically stable across K for K ≥ 25 (§5, K-eval ablation), consistent with each λ-slice having already converged; and (ii) although the asymptotic optimum-set is invariant to the precise sampling law (Remark 3 in §3), at finite training budget the law of p_λ matters for which slices receive enough gradient signal: log-uniform sampling on Burgers degrades rel-L² by a factor of ~14 relative to uniform at the matched 25k-epoch budget (§5.5). Remark 3 is therefore an asymptotic statement; finite-budget LC-PINN training is sensitive to p_λ through coverage rather than through the optimum-set.

**Limitations.** *Capacity*: a single network must fit the entire λ-family; on regions of Λ where solutions vary sharply (e.g., shock formation in BL across mobility ratios), the per-λ accuracy can lag the equal-budget single-λ PINN. *No solver data*: by construction LC-PINN cannot exploit precomputed high-fidelity data the way operator learners can; the comparison to FNO is therefore against a methodology that consumes a different kind of supervision. *Sampling distribution*: the prior p_λ governs which slices of the family the network learns well; for tail-heavy λ-priors the corresponding tail-slice predictions degrade. *Loss landscape*: the λ-conditioning interacts with multi-objective tradeoffs in the residual and BCs, and we do not have a sharp theoretical characterisation of which λ-priors are well-conditioned for a given PDE family.

**Future work.** Three directions are immediate. First, a principled prior over the λ-distribution: at present we use uniform or log-uniform, but the right prior for, say, a viscosity coefficient that varies across many decades is itself a question. Second, the spectral case: LC-PINN can be applied to eigenproblems via the Ky Fan variational form (initial experiments in our parallel work) and the loss-conditional structure becomes a conditioning-on-mode-index; that opens an entirely different family of problems. Third, combining the LC trick with operator learning: condition an FNO on λ to get an operator that amortises both over the function space of inputs *and* over a scalar physical parameter.
